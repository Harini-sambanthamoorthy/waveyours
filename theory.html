Microwave Transmission

Microwaves are electromagnetic waves in the 1 GHz to 1000 GHz range. They are primarily used in two configurations: Terrestrial and Satellite.

1. Terrestrial Microwave

Uses focused beams between ground-based antennas. Operates on Line-of-Sight (LoS) transmission.

  • Frequency Range: 4-6 GHz to 21-23 GHz.
  • Bandwidth: 1 to 10 Mbps.
  • Advantages: Cheaper than cables, no land acquisition needed, ideal for difficult terrains.
  • Disadvantages: Susceptible to weather, eavesdropping risks, and bandwidth limitations.

2. Satellite Microwave

Uses a physical object revolving around the earth at a known height to amplify and retransmit signals.

  • Mechanism: Signal is sent to the satellite (Up link), amplified, and sent back to another station (Down link).
  • Advantages: Massive coverage area, installation ease, and distance-independent transmission costs.
  • Timeline: Typical satellite life is 12-15 years, requiring planned launches for replacement.

Infrared (IR) Waves

IR transmission is a wireless technology for short-range communication, typically between 300 GHz and 400 THz.

Characteristics:

  • Privacy: Cannot penetrate walls, preventing interference from nearby rooms and providing high security.
  • Performance: Supports very high bandwidth and data rates.
  • Limitation: Unreliable outdoors due to interference from solar radiation (sun rays).
